Yamaha 9.9 — annual service (årlig service)

Annual service plan for a Yamaha 9.9 hp outboard, roughly 15 years old (≈ 2010–2012). In Europe an engine of that vintage is almost certainly the four-stroke F9.9 / FT9.9 (High Thrust) twin — the two-strokes disappeared from EU sale around 2007. Everything below is written for the four-stroke; a note at the end covers the two-stroke differences in case yours is one. This is the classic archipelago workhorse (aktersnurra): simple, robust, and happy to run for decades if the oil, gear oil and impeller are kept fresh.

Check the model plate first. The plate on the transom clamp bracket gives the exact model code (e.g. F9.9J, FT9.9G) and serial number. Part numbers for the impeller kit, oil filter, thermostat and anodes vary by model code — quote the code when buying parts, and treat the part numbers in this page as typical examples, not gospel.
Safety. Pull the kill-switch lanyard and disconnect the spark-plug caps (and battery, if electric start) before turning the flywheel or working near the propeller. Never run the engine without cooling water — even a few seconds dry ruins the impeller. Petrol and oily rags: keep away from flame, dispose of used oil at a collection point (problemavfall).

4. Step by step

4.1 Engine oil (do it warm)

  1. Run the engine to temperature in a water butt or on muffs — warm oil drains fully.
  2. Engine upright and level. Remove the dipstick, undo the drain screw low on the sump (or use a vacuum pump down the dipstick tube if access is poor on the bracket).
  3. Drain into a tray, new washer on the drain screw, snug it — it's a small thread, don't gorilla it.
  4. Swap the oil filter if fitted (light smear of oil on the new gasket, hand-tight + ¾ turn).
  5. Fill ≈ 0.8 L, wait a minute, check the dipstick, top to the upper mark. Run briefly, re-check, look for leaks.

4.2 Gear oil — fill from the bottom

  1. Engine upright, tray underneath. Open the vent screw (upper, on the side of the gear case) first, then the drain screw (lowest point, near the skeg).
  2. Inspect what comes out: honey-brown = good; grey/milky = water in the oil; glitter on the magnetic drain screw = gear wear. Water → renew the prop-shaft seal (or have it done) before the season.
  3. Pump new SAE 90 hypoid oil in through the bottom drain hole until it runs out of the upper vent hole with no bubbles.
  4. New washers: close the vent first, then swap the pump nozzle out for the drain screw quickly and close it. The vacuum holds the oil in while you swap.

4.3 Water pump impeller

  1. Shift into forward gear (you'll need the shift rod aligned for reassembly). Undo the lower-unit bolts (and the shift-rod connector per your model) and drop the leg straight down.
  2. Unbolt the water-pump housing on top of the gearbox; slide housing and impeller up off the driveshaft. Note the drive key/pin.
  3. Fit the new impeller with a little soap or glycerine; rotate the driveshaft clockwise while pressing the housing down so the vanes bend the right way. New gaskets/O-ring and wear plate from the kit.
  4. Refit the leg: grease the driveshaft splines (not the top face — grease there can hold the shaft proud and load the crank), align driveshaft, water tube and shift rod, bolt up.
  5. Test in a butt/on muffs immediately: strong telltale jet within seconds, or stop and re-check.

4.4 Fuel system

  1. Replace the inline fuel filter element; check the sediment bowl if fitted.
  2. Squeeze-test the primer bulb and flex all hoses — any cracking, stiffness or fuel smell → replace hose with marine-grade fuel line and new clamps. 15-year-old hose owes you nothing.
  3. Drain the carburettor float bowl via its drain screw (fuel tap off / line disconnected, engine off). Gum and green varnish here explain poor idling — a carb clean with new bowl gasket fixes most of it.
  4. Check the tank: water in the bottom, rusty cap seal, blocked vent. Fresh fuel every season; alkylate petrol if the engine sits for long spells.

4.5 Grease, anodes, propeller

  1. Grease gun on every nipple: swivel bracket, tilt tube, steering pivot. Unscrew the transom clamp screws fully, grease the threads, run them back.
  2. Anodes: remove, wire-brush the mating face bright, refit — or replace if ≥ half gone. Good metal-to-metal contact is the whole point.
  3. Prop off: straighten and pull the split pin, nut off, prop off. Unwind any fishing line at the seal (if you find line, watch the next gear-oil change for milkiness). Grease the spline, refit, torque the castle nut snug and fit a new split pin.

5. End of season (vinterförvaring)

  • Flush with fresh water 5–10 min (muffs or butt).
  • Run the carb dry (fuel tap off / line disconnected, let it stall) or fill with stabilised/alkylate fuel — pick one and be consistent.
  • Change engine oil and gear oil in autumn, not spring: acids and any water then don't sit in the engine all winter.
  • Pull the plugs, a small squirt of fogging oil or engine oil in each cylinder, turn the flywheel a few times by hand, plugs back loosely.
  • Store upright (or on the correct side per the manual — never tilt-side down, oil finds the cylinders), somewhere dry. The self-draining cooling system needs no antifreeze if stored upright and drained.
  • Battery (electric start): store charged and cool, trickle-charge over winter.
Spring is then trivial: plugs in and torqued, fresh fuel, battery on, check the telltale on first start — and the season begins.

6. If it turns out to be a two-stroke

A 9.9 two-stroke (pre-≈2007, or grey-import) skips the engine-oil and valve items entirely: no sump, no oil filter, no valve clearances. Instead: premix 1:50 with TC-W3 outboard oil (or keep the autolube tank clean and bled, if fitted), decarbonise the exhaust ports if power fades, and be extra fussy about the carburettor — two-strokes forgive dirty fuel even less. Gear oil, impeller, anodes, greasing, propeller and lay-up are exactly as above.

Rough budget (DIY): oil + gear oil + plugs + filters + washers ≈ 40–60 €; impeller kit ≈ 30–50 €; anodes ≈ 15–30 €. A dealer annual service on a 9.9 typically runs 200–350 € — the work above is 2–4 unhurried hours on a pair of sawhorses at the cabin.
