Saaristolaishuussi (family size) — 190 × 180 cm

A traditional Finnish archipelago privy (huussi) built entirely from solid wood — no plywood anywhere — now sized for a family: room for the Biolan Eco composting toilet, a child's potty + nappy-change bench, and storage shelves for paper, peat and sanitiser. Interior ≈ 3.1 m². Solid tongue-and-groove floorboards, vertical board-and-batten cladding (peiterimalaudoitus) in Falu red with white trim, and a hand-built ledged-and-braced plank door with a heart cut-out. The roof is a traditional Finnish bitumen-felt roof (huopakatto) — bitumen shingles (palahuopa) over an underlay felt (aluskate), all carried on a solid tongue-and-groove board deck (raakaponttilauta / umpilaudoitus), not plywood. It is a quiet, dark, weather-tight monopitch that looks right on the outer islands and is easy to patch for decades. (An earlier version used a translucent polycarbonate roof for overhead daylight; because a felt roof is opaque, the room is now lit by the side window — see the note under §5 for keeping it bright.)

Key dimensions — exterior 190 × 180 cm, interior ≈ 181 × 171 cm (3.1 m²). Front wall (door side) 220 cm, back wall 170 cm, measured from the raised floor; roof falls 50 cm over 180 cm ≈ 15.5° pitch. Door rough opening 80 × 195 cm, opens outward. Set on concrete piers or leveled on bedrock — the classic archipelago footing.
The toilet is recessed, it does not stand on the floor. Biolan's instructions are clear: the eco unit is 97 cm tall and about half of it must sit below the floor so the seat ends up at a normal ≈ 48–50 cm. So this privy has a raised floor ~48 cm above the ground. You cut a hole in the floor (to Biolan's sawing template), drop the unit through so its lower half sits in the enclosed space underneath, and a hatch in the back skirt lets you slide the container out to empty it. You step up ~48 cm to get in. All wall heights below are measured from the raised floor — overall height above ground is 48 cm more.

4. Building the door yourself

The door is the part you build by hand, the old way: a ledged-and-braced plank door (kehäponttiovi / vinotukiovi) — vertical boards held together by three horizontal ledges and two diagonal braces on the back. No frame-and-panel joinery, no plywood. Drawn from the inside face.

5. Roof structure & decking plan (top view)

Losing the daylight roof — plan the light. The old polycarbonate roof lit the room from above; a felt roof is opaque, so daylight now comes only from the side window. Keep that window (a reclaimed cottage sash suits the look), and consider one of: a second small window in the door or front wall, a glazed or louvred strip above the door, or a single clear/opal polycarbonate rooflight panel let into one deck bay if you still want a little light from above while keeping the felt roof everywhere else.

6. Wall framing (stud layout)

Stick-framed walls in 45 × 95 mm (studs at ~600 mm centres), a bottom plate and a doubled top plate. The two side walls' top plates are cut on the 15.5° slope. Openings are framed the standard way: king studs full-height each side, jack (trimmer) studs carrying a header, plus a rough sill and cripple studs at the window.

Cladding nailing. Vertical board-and-batten can't nail to vertical studs, so add horizontal furring battens (22 × 50 mm) across the studs at ~600 mm — they give the fixing for the vertical boards and form the ventilation gap behind the cladding. (No breather membrane — unnecessary on an uninsulated, unlined outbuilding.) Frame a small nogging wherever a shelf or grab rail will be screwed to the wall. Fixing pattern for wood movement — see the detail below.

7. Foundation & floor framing

The hut sits on 9 concrete pier blocks (pilariharkko, 240 × 240 × 195 mm) in a 3 × 3 grid, leveled on crushed stone or bedrock. Short PT posts (cut individually to level the building on the rock) carry three bearers running front-to-back, and six floor joists run across them. So no joist has to be cut, the Biolan drops through a 62 cm clear bay at the back-left: five joists sit toward the front at ~270 mm centres, and a doubled trimmer joist leaves a 62 cm gap to the back rim. Floor top lands at ≈ 48 cm above grade so the toilet recesses.

No plywood — a solid board deck instead. A felt roof needs a continuous, stiff surface, and the usual choice is plywood or OSB. To keep this build all-solid-timber, deck it with 28 × 120 mm tongue-and-groove roof board (raakaponttilauta) run tight across the rafters — every board end must land on a rafter. Roll the underlay felt up-slope with generous laps, then nail the bitumen shingles from a doubled starter course at the low (back) eave upward, folding the felt over the edge boards at the verges and the high front edge. At the 15.5° pitch (≈ 1:3.6) shingles sit comfortably above their ~1:5 minimum; if you prefer the old roll-felt look, kolmiorimahuopa (roll felt over triangular battens) works on the same deck.

9. Build sequence (short version)

  1. Site & footings (see §7). Level the 9 pilariharkko in a 3 × 3 grid on crushed stone/rock. Set short PT posts on each, cut to bring the whole frame level (and to the +48 cm floor height — the blocks + frame do most of the lift, so posts are only ~5–10 cm).
  2. Floor frame & deck. Run 3 PT bearers front-to-back on the posts, then PT joists at 400 mm OC across them with a rim joist all round; frame the toilet opening (back-left) with doubled headers. Deck with the thermowood. Dry-fit the Biolan, mark and cut the opening to its template so the seat lands at ~48–50 cm.
  3. Walls. Frame four panels (heights from the raised floor): front & back rectangular; side walls' top plate cut on the 15.5° slope (front 220 → back 170). Frame the side-wall window opening (in the right wall), sill ~85 cm, toward the back at the seat's depth. Stand, plumb, screw corners, add the lapping top plate.
  4. Roof structure & deck. Set the 4 rafters (45×120) front-to-back at ~600 mm; tie down with straps. Overhang ~20 front, ~30 back, 15 each side. Then lay the solid T&G board deck (28×120) tight across the rafters — no plywood — landing every board end on a rafter. Fit the wooden fascia/edge boards at eaves and verges.
  5. Felt roof (huopakatto). Roll the underlay felt (aluskate) up the slope over the whole deck with generous laps and nail it off. Lay a doubled starter course at the low (back) eave, then nail the bitumen shingles (palahuopa) course by course up to the high front edge, staggering the tabs. Fold the felt over the edge boards at the two verges and the high edge; bed those laps and the tabs in shingle mastic. If the Biolan vent goes through the roof, seal it with a pipe collar (otherwise it runs up the back wall).
  6. Window, skirt & hatch. Bed the side-wall window, seal and trim it. Clad the ~48 cm under-floor skirt down to the ground and build the hinged access hatch (with insect screen) into the back skirt.
  7. Cladding & trim. Vertical 22×120 boards, then 22×45 battens over the gaps. White corner boards, door & window surrounds, fascia. Seal the wall-to-roof junction.
  8. Door (see §4). Build the ledged-and-braced door from seven 22×120 boards; hang on three strap hinges to swing outward; latch, privacy hook, cut the heart.
  9. Fit-out. Build the right-wall bench + shelves; add the peat bin, roll holder and hooks. Steps up to the door (or a flat rock).
  10. Toilet & vent. Lower the Biolan through the floor hole onto its base; fit the liquid-separator plate. Vent up the back wall above the roofline; add the screened wall vent up high.
  11. Finish. Two coats of Falu red on all cladding (walls + skirt), white on trim and window; caulk the door, window and roof junctions.
Three things to plan for with the felt roof:
  • Daylight. A felt roof is opaque, so the bright-from-above quality of the old polycarbonate roof is gone. Lean on the side window and, if you want, add a second small window (door or front wall) or a single clear/opal rooflight panel in one deck bay — see the note under §5.
  • A stiff, dry deck. Shingles telegraph every dip, so lay the T&G boards tight with end-joints landing on rafters, and let the timber dry before felting. On this uninsulated, open-ceilinged hut there is no warm-side moisture driving into the deck, so a simple cold deck is fine — just keep the wall vents open for through-flow.
  • Edges & low pitch. At 15.5° the eaves and verge laps do the work — double the starter course, wrap and mastic the felt over the edge boards, and (optionally) tuck a metal drip edge under the underlay at the low eave so wind-driven rain can't track back under the shingles.
