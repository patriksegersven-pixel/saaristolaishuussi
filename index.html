Saaristolaishuussi (family size) — 190 × 180 cm

A traditional Finnish archipelago privy (huussi) built entirely from solid wood — no plywood anywhere — now sized for a family: room for the Biolan Eco composting toilet, a child's potty + nappy-change bench, and storage shelves for paper, peat and sanitiser. Interior ≈ 3.1 m². Solid tongue-and-groove floorboards, vertical board-and-batten cladding (peiterimalaudoitus) in Falu red with white trim, and a hand-built ledged-and-braced plank door with a heart cut-out. The one modern twist: the entire roof is a single translucent surface of 16 mm opal (white-translucent) twin-wall polycarbonate, so soft daylight floods the room and there is almost nothing overhead to collect dust.

Key dimensions — exterior 190 × 180 cm, interior ≈ 181 × 171 cm (3.1 m²). Front wall (door side) 220 cm, back wall 170 cm, measured from the raised floor; roof falls 50 cm over 180 cm ≈ 15.5° pitch. Door rough opening 80 × 195 cm, opens outward. Set on concrete piers or leveled on bedrock — the classic archipelago footing.
The toilet is recessed, it does not stand on the floor. Biolan's instructions are clear: the eco unit is 97 cm tall and about half of it must sit below the floor so the seat ends up at a normal ≈ 48–50 cm. So this privy has a raised floor ~48 cm above the ground. You cut a hole in the floor (to Biolan's sawing template), drop the unit through so its lower half sits in the enclosed space underneath, and a hatch in the back skirt lets you slide the container out to empty it. You step up ~48 cm to get in. All wall heights below are measured from the raised floor — overall height above ground is 48 cm more.

4. Building the door yourself

The door is the part you build by hand, the old way: a ledged-and-braced plank door (kehäponttiovi / vinotukiovi) — vertical boards held together by three horizontal ledges and two diagonal braces on the back. No frame-and-panel joinery, no plywood. Drawn from the inside face.

6. Wall framing (stud layout)

Stick-framed walls in 45 × 95 mm (studs at ~600 mm centres), a bottom plate and a doubled top plate. The two side walls' top plates are cut on the 15.5° slope. Openings are framed the standard way: king studs full-height each side, jack (trimmer) studs carrying a header, plus a rough sill and cripple studs at the window.

Cladding nailing. Vertical board-and-batten can't nail to vertical studs, so add horizontal furring battens (22 × 50 mm) across the studs at ~600 mm — they give the fixing for the vertical boards and form the ventilation gap behind the cladding. (No breather membrane — unnecessary on an uninsulated, unlined outbuilding.) Frame a small nogging wherever a shelf or grab rail will be screwed to the wall. Fixing pattern for wood movement — see the detail below.

7. Foundation & floor framing

The hut sits on 9 concrete pier blocks (pilariharkko, 240 × 240 × 195 mm) in a 3 × 3 grid, leveled on crushed stone or bedrock. Short PT posts (cut individually to level the building on the rock) carry three bearers running front-to-back, and floor joists at 400 mm centres run across them. The Biolan opening in the back-right corner is framed with doubled joists and headers. Floor top lands at ≈ 48 cm above grade so the toilet recesses.

Lumber sizes are nominal milled dimensions (Nordic/European: 45 × 95 mm ≈ US 2×4; 95 × 95 mm ≈ US 4×4). Quantities include a small waste allowance and are scaled for the 190 × 180 cm family size.

No plywood anywhere. Floor, walls, door and shelving are all solid sawn timber. Finnish lumberyard names are in brackets (K-Rauta / Stark / Bauhaus).

All-wood glazing — no aluminium. The polycarbonate sits on the wooden rafters over a strip of EPDM/foam gasket tape; a planed wood cap batten is then screwed down over each rafter (gasket tape under it too) to clamp and weatherproof the sheet edges and the centre joint. Sheet ends get flute tape tucked under a slim wood end-cap, and a bevelled wood board forms the eave drip. Use stainless gasketed screws, drill the sheet's screw holes oversize and don't over-tighten, so the polycarbonate can expand and contract under the wood without buckling.

Ventilation, fasteners, finish

Salt air = stainless. Use A4/A2 stainless or hot-dip galvanised screws and hinges throughout — ordinary zinc bleeds rust streaks down the cladding within a season.

9. Build sequence (short version)

  1. Site & footings (see §7). Level the 9 pilariharkko in a 3 × 3 grid on crushed stone/rock. Set short PT posts on each, cut to bring the whole frame level (and to the +48 cm floor height — the blocks + frame do most of the lift, so posts are only ~5–10 cm).
  2. Floor frame & deck. Run 3 PT bearers front-to-back on the posts, then PT joists at 400 mm OC across them with a rim joist all round; frame the toilet opening (back-right) with doubled headers. Deck with the thermowood. Dry-fit the Biolan, mark and cut the opening to its template so the seat lands at ~48–50 cm.
  3. Walls. Frame four panels (heights from the raised floor): front & back rectangular; side walls' top plate cut on the 15.5° slope (front 220 → back 170). Frame the side-wall window opening (the wall on your right when seated), sill ~85 cm, toward the back at the seat's depth. Stand, plumb, screw corners, add the lapping top plate.
  4. Roof structure. Set the 3 rafters (45×120) front-to-back at ~110 cm, plus the single mid purlin; tie down with straps. Overhang ~20 front, ~30 back, 15 each side.
  5. Glazing (all wood). Run EPDM/foam gasket tape along each rafter, lay the two PC panels flutes-down-slope, then screw a planed wood cap batten over each rafter (gasket tape under it, stainless gasketed screws, sheet holes oversize). Tape the sheet ends (solid high, vented low) under slim wood end-caps; fit the wooden drip board. Don't over-tighten — let the sheet move.
  6. Window, skirt & hatch. Bed the side-wall window, seal and trim it. Clad the ~48 cm under-floor skirt down to the ground and build the hinged access hatch (with insect screen) into the back skirt.
  7. Cladding & trim. Vertical 22×120 boards, then 22×45 battens over the gaps. White corner boards, door & window surrounds, fascia. Seal the wall-to-roof junction.
  8. Door (see §4). Build the ledged-and-braced door from seven 22×120 boards; hang on three strap hinges to swing outward; latch, privacy hook, cut the heart.
  9. Fit-out. Build the right-wall bench + shelves; add the peat bin, roll holder and hooks. Steps up to the door (or a flat rock).
  10. Toilet & vent. Lower the Biolan through the floor hole onto its base; fit the liquid-separator plate. Vent up the back wall above the roofline; add the screened wall vent up high.
  11. Finish. Two coats of Falu red on all cladding (walls + skirt), white on trim and window; caulk the door, window and roof junctions.
Two things to plan for with an all-clear roof:
  • Heat & glare — handled. You've specced opal (white-translucent) sheet: it floods the room with soft daylight but diffuses the sun, cuts glare and heat, and gives overhead privacy — the right call with kids. Still give it good cross-flow on hot days via the wall vents + open door.
  • Condensation. Twin-wall drains internally only if the flutes run down-slope and the low end is vented (hence the vented tape on the back edge). Keep that orientation.
